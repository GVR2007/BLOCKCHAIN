Section 01
Why Don't People Use the Toilets That Are Already Built?

India's Swachh Bharat Mission and Nirmal Bharat Abhiyan constructed millions of toilets. But construction alone does not equal usage. Field observations reveal that toilets remain underutilized not just because of habit — but because of a complex mix of practical, infrastructural, and behavioral barriers. The new abstract identifies six distinct root causes:

💧

Water scarcity reduces willingness to use toilets

In water-scarce areas, households prioritize drinking and cooking over flushing. Every litre used for sanitation feels like a sacrifice. Traditional open defecation requires zero water — making it feel more "efficient" in resource-stressed conditions.

📐

Poor toilet design and insufficient space

Many government-built toilets have cramped interiors, inadequate ventilation, no water storage provisions, and layouts that don't match how local families actually use sanitation. Structural issues make them uncomfortable or even unusable for many.

🔧

Irregular maintenance and structural deficiencies

Once built, many toilets receive no follow-up maintenance. Cracked walls, broken doors, blocked pits, and absent lighting are common. A dysfunctional toilet quickly becomes abandoned, reinforcing the preference for open defecation.

🧠

Existing open defecation habits are deeply ingrained

Behavioral change is slow. Decades of outdoor defecation create strong habits tied to social routines (group outings, early morning customs). These are not easily broken by infrastructure alone — behavior change needs active, sustained motivation.

🪞

Perceptions of cleanliness favor the outdoors

Paradoxically, many rural households perceive the open field as cleaner than an enclosed toilet. Fear of smell, flies, and enclosed spaces — combined with the idea that defecating near the home is unclean — creates a perception barrier that policy alone cannot fix.

📚

Limited awareness of long-term health benefits

Without regular health education, communities don't connect open defecation to diarrhea, cholera, typhoid, and stunting in children. The long-term health cost is invisible; the short-term convenience of open defecation feels immediate and real.

→

This framework addresses all six barriers simultaneously through four integrated pillars

Behavioral incentives (tokens) tackle motivation. Maintenance reporting tackles infrastructure. Water-efficient design guidance tackles scarcity. Community tracking tackles social norms. Blockchain ensures all of it is transparent and tamper-proof.

Section 02
The Four Pillars of the Framework

Unlike previous approaches that focused only on building toilets or only on awareness campaigns, this framework operates on four simultaneous pillars — each targeting a different type of barrier. All four are connected through the blockchain backbone.

🪙
Behavioral Incentives
Digital token rewards for regular, verified toilet usage. Immediate, tangible benefit that shifts the cost-benefit calculation away from open defecation toward consistent sanitation facility use.
⛓
Transparent Monitoring
All usage data and resource recovery metrics recorded on blockchain — tamper-proof, real-time, and accessible to government, community leaders, and households themselves. No fake reports possible.
💧
Water-Efficient Sanitation
Guidance and design support for low-flush, pour-flush, and dry sanitation options that drastically reduce water needed per use. The system rewards water-efficient practices, removing the scarcity burden.
🏘
Community Accountability
Village-level dashboards show community performance rankings, maintenance alerts, and collective progress toward ODF targets. Peer visibility creates social motivation and collective ownership of sanitation outcomes.
🔑 Why social acceptance is the key focus: Previous frameworks treated sanitation as an engineering problem. This framework treats it as a social acceptance problem. A toilet is only useful when people choose to use it voluntarily, consistently, and with dignity. The blockchain reward system makes that choice feel rewarding rather than burdensome — turning a public health imperative into a personally beneficial habit.

Section 05
Step-by-Step: How the System Works in Daily Life

Forget the technology for a moment. Imagine you are a household in a rural village. Here is exactly what your day looks like — and where the system touches your life.

1
🌅Morning: A Family Member Uses the Toilet

A household member walks to the toilet. The toilet has a small IoT sensor attached (looks like a smoke detector). As the toilet is used, the sensor detects the usage event — either through a flow meter (water usage), a door-open/close sensor, or a weight plate trigger. The usage is logged with a timestamp and the household's unique ID. Crucially, in water-scarce homes, the system is configured for low-flush or pour-flush toilets — needing as little as 1–2 litres per use vs. 6–10 litres for a regular flush toilet.

2
🔧Any Time: Reporting a Toilet Issue via the App

If the toilet door hinge breaks, the pit is full, or the roof leaks — the household opens the app, takes a photo, taps the issue type, and submits. The report is written to the blockchain with a timestamp. The panchayat officer gets an automatic notification. A community health volunteer is dispatched to verify the issue. Once the repair is done and the volunteer marks it resolved on-chain, the household earns a "maintenance participation" token bonus. The whole process is traceable — nobody can claim a repair happened if it didn't.

Maintenance reporting flow
Household opens app → taps "Report Issue" → selects category (structural / water / cleanliness / other)
Photo upload + optional voice note → auto-tagged with GPS + household ID → written to blockchain
Panchayat dashboard shows alert → assigns repair team → deadline set (3 days)
Repair completed → community volunteer verifies on-app → blockchain entry: "Resolved"
Household earns 15 GT bonus for participating → panchayat scored on resolution time
Unresolved issues escalate automatically to block/district level after deadline
3
🏘Weekly: Community Leaderboard Updates

Every week, the system calculates a village-level ODF score — a composite of average toilet usage rate, maintenance responsiveness, water efficiency, and health module completion. This score is displayed on a public notice board (printed weekly by the gram panchayat) AND in the app. Villages that cross the 90% usage threshold get a community reward — a shared fund that the village can vote to spend on a public good (clean water point, school supplies, streetlight). This makes sanitation a community achievement, not just a household chore.

Why community scoring works: Research in CLTS (Community-Led Total Sanitation) shows that peer visibility is one of the most powerful triggers for behavior change in rural India. When a household knows that their neighbor can see their village's score dropping because of OD, social pressure activates. This framework automates that visibility and makes it positive and rewarding — rather than shaming.
4
📜Automatic: Smart Contract Calculates and Issues Tokens

Every night at midnight, the smart contract runs automatically. It reads all usage events, water-efficiency readings, maintenance reports submitted, and health module completions from the day. Using a pre-set formula (coded in Solidity, audited and locked — nobody can change it), it calculates each household's token earnings and sends them directly to their wallet. No human approves it. No official can delay it. No corruption is possible.

5
🛒Redeeming GreenTokens for Real Benefits

The household opens their mobile wallet app. They see their GT balance, a history of how each token was earned, and a redemption menu. Redemption is supported by government policy — meaning these tokens are accepted at the local ration shop, the LPG gas distributor, and the primary health center, just like a government coupon. This tangible, immediate value is what makes behavior change stick in the long run.

Section 06
Addressing Water Scarcity — A Special Focus

The new abstract specifically highlights water scarcity as a key barrier — one the original framework didn't fully address. Here's how the updated system tackles it at three levels.

🔩 Hardware level

Low-flush adapters retrofit existing toilet cisterns to use 2–3L per flush instead of 8–10L.

Pour-flush sensor detects a pour-flush event (jug of water used directly) and logs it as a valid usage — no cistern needed.

Dry-pit sensor for fully waterless eco-sanitation units in the most water-stressed areas — detects pit usage via weight sensor with zero water requirement.

📱 Software level

Water efficiency dashboard shows each household how many litres they saved this week vs. a standard flush baseline.

Bonus tokens awarded per litre saved — making water-efficient toilet use more rewarding than regular flushing.

Seasonal adjustment: during water-stress months (March–May in Tamil Nadu), token weights for water-saving behaviour automatically increase, acknowledging the greater sacrifice.

🏘 Community level

Village water-saved leaderboard — total community litres saved per month displayed on community board. Collective milestone (e.g., 10,000 litres saved) triggers a community water storage facility grant.

Rainwater harvesting integration: panchayats that link toilet water supply to a rooftop rainwater tank get an infrastructure grant coded into the smart contract — triggered when tank installation is verified on-chain.

"In a pour-flush toilet, a household member uses a jug of 1–2 litres of water to flush waste — compared to 6–10 litres in a cistern flush. Over a family of five using the toilet twice a day, this saves up to 80 litres per day — equivalent to 4 large buckets of water that can now go to drinking or cooking."

— Design rationale: UNICEF/WHO pour-flush toilet standards for South Asia
Section 07
Technology Stack — What Powers the System

Seven layers of technology work together. Each one is chosen for reliability in low-resource, low-connectivity rural Indian environments.

Layer 1 — Physical Sensing
🔧 IoT Hardware
Raspberry Pi / ESP32 sensor nodes with flow meters, ultrasonic distance sensors (for pour-flush detection), MQ-4 gas sensors (biogas), door-open reed switches, and weight pads. Low-power (~3W), weatherproof casing. Cost: ~₹2,800/unit.
ESP32 · LoRa · RS-485
Layer 2 — Connectivity
📶 Resilient Transmission
MQTT over 4G/WiFi with LoRaWAN fallback for areas with no cellular. Offline-first: stores up to 7 days of data locally on the sensor node if internet fails, syncs automatically when reconnected. Works on 2G GPRS in most remote cases.
MQTT · LoRaWAN · AWS IoT
Layer 3 — Blockchain
⛓ Distributed Ledger
Hyperledger Fabric (government-permissioned nodes at block, district, and state level) or Polygon PoS (public chain, low gas fees). All usage, maintenance, community, and water-efficiency data stored immutably. ~3s confirmation time.
Hyperledger Fabric · Polygon · IPFS
Layer 4 — Incentive Logic
📜 Smart Contracts
ERC-20 GreenToken (GT) contracts coded in Solidity. Multi-variable reward formula: usage + water efficiency + resource recovery + maintenance + health education. Audited, open-source. Anti-gaming: anomaly detection flags suspicious sensor spikes.
Solidity · ERC-20 · Chainlink
Layer 5 — Application
📱 Mobile App + Portals
React Native Android app (Tamil/Telugu/Hindi, voice-guided for low-literacy users). Features: wallet, issue reporting, health modules, community score, redemption QR codes. Government admin web portal shows real-time district-wide sanitation dashboard.
React Native · Node.js · GraphQL
Layer 6 — Community Interface
🏘 Community Dashboard
Weekly printed report (auto-generated PDF from blockchain data, printed at panchayat office) for households without smartphones. Digital village kiosk version for gram sabha meetings. Village ODF score, top households, water saved this month, pending maintenance issues.
Python · LibreOffice · Grafana
Layer 7 — Analytics
📊 Data Intelligence
ML model predicts which households are at risk of reverting to OD (based on usage frequency drop) and triggers targeted outreach. Correlation analysis of sanitation data vs. local health records submitted to government quarterly.
Python · TensorFlow Lite · PostgreSQL
